Introduction

The last updated Motor Trend Car Road Test occured in 1974 for R, (mtcars in R). Ive created a new analysis using the US goverment data from http://www.fueleconomy.gov/feg/epadata/vehicles.csv.zip. This includes most makes in models from 1985 up to 2017. The nice aspect of this data, is that it includes some more modern vehicles like the bmw i3 BEV, which gets 138 miles in the city. This data does not include vehicle weight, which is disappointing. Previously it has been shown there is a correlation between weight of veheicle and miles per gallon. There are over 37,000 vehicles in this data set.

Background of data

The data includes make-model-year for most cars sold in the United States from 1985 to 1974. This does include some vehicles that are classified as gass guzlers and more noteworthy vehicles that can reach 120mpg. I checked to see how accurate the miles per gallon (mpg) is, and for my car and my spouces car, it holds up. For this file comb08 is the combination of highway miles and city miles.

Analysis

Numerous models were analysed. I looked at all the data together, separating data with more than 30mpg and less than 30mpg, and vehicles after 2010, the first year there is a jump in mpg. I noticed, for all vehicles, manual has a higher mean than automatic. I think this is partly due to automatic at one time having better milage performance, but at sometime, the technology switched and automatic performed better.The top performing models are all automatic now, and this has skewed the data, but overall the data takes on a gausian distribution.

Executive Summary

From the data, it like transmission makes a different, but it is not the most important. I looked at the logistic regression for 1. transmission_automatic 2. transmission_manual 3. transmission + year 4. transmission + cylinders 5. transmission + years + cylinders 6. cylinders 7. year From the data, the coefficient and Rsquared were most significant for year and cylinders in describing mpg on a car. It would make sense for the regression line to go threw the origin, it would not make sense for anything to have a negative miles per gallon. Cylinders and year impact performance more than transmission type. This seems to go in hand with technology increasing milage performance. Overall, it looks like cylinders, year and transmission account for between 55% and 90% of the variation in miles per gallon.
